What is Industrial Processing?

Industrial Control Systems (ICS) are designed to control variables in the physical world, while IT systems are designed to manage data.

Aspect ICS Systems IT Systems
Primary Priority Availability & Data Integrity Confidentiality

Operating Environment Harsh industrial conditions Climate-controlled offices
Interface Industrial I/O ports for sensors/actuators User-friendly keyboards, mice, displays

Key Requirements for Industrial Process Controllers

Availability

Many processes are continuous operations requiring high availability, reliability, and maintainability ratings.

Timeliness

Process control requires quick response times. Front-line controllers operate on real-time operating systems.

Industrial Interfacing

Controllers provide industrial-style input/output ports for connecting sensors and actuators rather than user interfaces.

Physical Hardening

Designed to operate in harsh environments like factories and outdoor installations.

Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs)

PLCs are the most common field devices in industrial and utility process control systems.

Key PLC Module Types

  • Power Supply Unit (PSU): Supplies power (typically 24Vac) to CPU and I/O modules through the backplane
  • Central Processing Unit (CPU): Contains microprocessor, RAM/ROM memory, I/O interfacing, and communications support
  • Input Modules: Analog inputs (continuous variable signals) and Digital inputs (two-state/on-off signals)
  • Output Modules: Analog outputs (variable control signals) and Digital outputs (two-position actuators)
  • Communication Modules: Enable communication with other devices via RS-232, RS-485, Ethernet, or wireless protocols

Remote Telemetry Units (RTUs)

Telemetry: The process of using sensors to collect information in a remote location and transmitting it to another location for processing.

RTU Characteristics

  • Small intelligent control units deployed at selective locations within a process
  • Gather data from different sensors
  • Deliver commands to control relay outputs
  • Ideal for geographically distributed systems
  • Often used in oil & gas pipelines, water distribution, and electrical substations

Distributed Control Systems (DCS)

When processes become too complex for a single controller, or components are geographically separated, distributed control is necessary.

DCS Components

  • Multiple Controllers: Distribute control functions across several devices
  • Supervisory Layer: Coordinates activities between controllers
  • Engineering Workstation: Programs field devices for current operations
  • Data Historian: Database that collects and stores process values for inspection and analysis
  • Human-Machine Interface (HMI): Enables operators to observe and control processes

SCADA Systems

Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition

A SCADA system provides data acquisition and supervisory control functions for distributed processes.

SCADA Functions

  • Data Acquisition: Collects data from field devices (PLCs, RTUs, IEDs)
  • Supervisory Control: Sends control commands to field devices
  • Visualization: Provides HMI for operators to observe and control processes
  • Historical Storage: Stores control points and process data in databases

SCADA systems are commonly used in utilities, oil & gas, water treatment, and manufacturing to monitor and control geographically dispersed assets.

Industrial Network Architecture

Four Distinct Network Segments

  • Field Bus Loop: Direct interaction between local field devices, sensors, and actuators. Connects PLCs to input/output devices.
  • Local Control/Supervisory Loop: Connects field devices to DCS components (Data Historian, Engineering Station, HMI, Supervisory Computers).
  • Organization's Local Area Network: Provides connectivity between IT network and ICS network. Represents potential security boundary.
  • Enterprise Network: Connects local IT network to wide area network. Creates pathway for external access and potential security risks.

Industrial Communication Protocols

Traditional Protocols

  • Serial Communications: RS-232 and RS-485 for point-to-point and multi-drop connections
  • Fieldbus Protocols: Proprietary protocols for sensor/actuator communication

Modern Protocols

  • Ethernet TCP/IP: Standard networking becoming prevalent in ICS
  • Wireless Technologies: Bluetooth, Zigbee, WiMAX for specific applications

Important: Smart IED devices must communicate using the same protocol as other devices on the field bus. Non-intelligent sensors still require independent wire runs to controllers.

The Purdue Model (ISA 95)

A five-level integration process standard for industrial networks:

  • Level 0 - Physical Process: The actual physical process being controlled (pumps, valves, motors)
  • Level 1 - Intelligent Devices: Sensors, actuators, and protection devices that directly interact with the process
  • Level 2 - Control Systems: PLCs, DCS, and SCADA systems that provide automated control
  • Level 3 - Operations Management: HMI, Engineering Workstations, Data Historians for workflow control
  • Level 4 - Business Systems: Plant scheduling, ERP systems, and business logistics

This model helps organizations understand security zones and appropriate segmentation strategies.

Industrial Safety & Protection Systems

Automated systems specifically designed to monitor and control unsafe or potentially unsafe conditions.

Key Safety System Types

  • Safety Instrumented Systems (SIS): Prevent hazardous events through independent protective actions
  • Emergency Shutdown Systems (ESD): Minimize consequences of emergency conditions
  • Fire & Gas Systems (FGS): Detect and respond to fire or gas release
  • Leak Detection Systems: Identify fluid or gas leaks in pipelines and vessels
  • Burner Management Systems (BMS): Control combustion processes safely
  • Vibration Monitoring: Detect mechanical issues in rotating equipment

Safety Integrity Levels (SIL)

SIL ratings indicate the reliability and safety level of protective systems:

SIL 1

Lowest safety level; highest probability of failure. Used for low-risk applications.

SIL 2

Increased safety level; decreased failure probability. More complex and expensive than SIL 1.

SIL 3

Highest practical rating. Expensive to acquire and maintain; often triggers process redesign.

SIL 4

Highest possible safety level. Too complex/costly for most industries; indicates design problems.

Week 1 Summary

Key Takeaways

  • ICS systems prioritize availability and timeliness over confidentiality
  • PLCs are the workhorses of industrial control, RTUs handle remote operations
  • DCS distributes control across multiple controllers; SCADA provides supervisory oversight
  • Industrial networks consist of distinct layers from field devices to enterprise systems
  • The Purdue Model (ISA 95) provides a framework for understanding industrial network architecture
  • Safety systems are independent protective layers with specific integrity ratings (SIL)
